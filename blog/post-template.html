Why Most Strategic Plans Fail — And How to Fix Yours

Dragana Radic May 8, 2026 5 min read
Back to all articles

Every year, countless organizations invest significant time and money into strategic planning. Leadership teams disappear into off-sites, consultants fill whiteboards with frameworks, and thick documents get produced. Then, about six months later, most of those plans are sitting untouched in someone's filing cabinet.

The problem isn't that people can't write good strategies. The problem is the gap between the document and the doing.

Why Plans Stall

In my experience working with organizations across multiple industries, strategic plans fail for a handful of recurring reasons. They're written at too high a level of abstraction. They lack clear ownership. They don't account for the reality of how people spend their time. And they rarely include a mechanism for reviewing progress honestly.

The result? A strategy that everyone agrees with in theory and nobody acts on in practice.

"A strategy that lives only in a document is not a strategy — it's a hope dressed up in bullet points."

A Framework That Actually Works

Over the years, I've refined a simple approach that dramatically improves the odds of a strategic plan getting executed. It comes down to four principles:

  1. Translate goals into decisions. Every strategic objective should be connected to specific decisions that need to be made. Who decides? By when? Based on what information?
  2. Assign owners, not teams. When everyone is responsible, nobody is. Every action in your plan should have a single named person accountable for it.
  3. Build in friction points. Schedule quarterly reviews where the honest question is: "Is this still the right strategy?" Give people permission to say it's not working.
  4. Connect strategy to daily work. If your team can't explain how their daily tasks connect to the strategic goals, the strategy isn't real yet.

Where to Start

If you're looking at a strategic plan right now that's not gaining traction, I'd suggest starting with a single question: What is the one decision, if made in the next 30 days, that would have the biggest impact on whether this strategy succeeds?

Find that decision. Find the person who needs to make it. Remove the obstacles in their way. Everything else follows from there.

Strategy isn't about having the right answer on paper. It's about building the organizational capacity to make good decisions, consistently, over time. That's the work — and it's worth doing well.
